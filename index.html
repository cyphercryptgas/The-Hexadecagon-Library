About the Stacks

This is a walkable reading room for the world's published books. The shelves are drawn live from the Open Library catalog — an open project of the Internet Archive holding records for tens of millions of works, the closest thing that exists to a complete public index of everything in print.

How the stacks are arranged

Sixty-four subjects form a ring of galleries: walk through a doorway and you pass to the next subject. Each gallery holds 640 volumes — four walls, five shelves, thirty-two books. The spiral stair moves you one page deeper or shallower within every subject at once: shallow galleries hold the most-published works, and the deeper you descend, the rarer the books become.

Spines and covers

Each spine carries its book's actual cover art, compressed the way a real spine compresses a jacket. Approach a shelf and the covers resolve; open a book for the full jacket, the publication facts, and the description.

Reading

Millions of these books are scanned, in three tiers. Full text: public-domain works open complete, every page. Preview: in-copyright scans show a limited preview here; a free archive.org account lets you borrow the whole book — that gate is copyright law, not a missing key. Metadata only: no scan exists anywhere yet. Each book is labeled before you open it, and the English-language edition is preferred where one exists.

A living library

Unlike its impossible sibling, this library changes — but on its own terms. When the self-hosted index is connected, the shelves are pinned to a monthly snapshot of the catalog (named on the landing page), so every address holds until the next snapshot. The durable bookmark across snapshots is the book itself — its ISBN or Open Library ID.

Lineage

Catalog data, covers, and reading are provided by Open Library and the Internet Archive; this explorer is independent and unaffiliated. It shares its architecture with a companion piece: a true-scale Library of Babel containing every possible book. Between the two libraries lies the difference between everything that could be written and everything that has been.
