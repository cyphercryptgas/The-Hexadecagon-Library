About the Library

This is a walkable reading room for the world's published books. The shelves are drawn live from the Open Library catalog — an open project of the Internet Archive holding records for tens of millions of works, the closest thing that exists to a complete public index of everything in print.

How the stacks are arranged

Sixty-four subjects form a ring of galleries: walk through a doorway and you pass to the next subject. Each gallery holds 640 volumes — four walls, five shelves, thirty-two books. The spiral stair is walkable: a full turn down carries you one page deeper within every subject at once. Shallow galleries hold the most-published works; the deeper you descend, the rarer the books become — until a subject runs out, and the empty shelves below wait for books not yet written.

The structure

The whole library is a ring of sixteen galleries that close into a hexadecagon, with levels descending beneath and a spiral stair threading every vestibule. Turn it, tip it, and look:

What stands on the shelves

The main ring is drawn live from Open Library — records for tens of millions of published books, with covers and scans from the Internet Archive, full public-domain texts from Project Gutenberg, and human-read audiobooks from LibriVox. Beyond the ring, three wings open through doorways:

The Research Wing — the most-cited scholarship across every field, from OpenAlex; open college textbooks from OpenStax; and tens of thousands of peer-reviewed open-access academic books from the Directory of Open Access Books. Papers open in full from arXiv.

The Law Library — federal and state case law (court opinions from the Supreme Court, every federal circuit, and the high courts of fourteen states), the written law itself (the United States Code, the Code of Federal Regulations, and the federal Public Laws as Congress passes them), and federal dockets — the lawsuits themselves, grouped by kind, with their parties and filing history. Opinions and dockets come from CourtListener / the RECAP Archive; statutes and regulations from govinfo.gov (the U.S. Government Publishing Office).

The Music Wing — in progress: public-domain scores and historical recordings.

Honest about the edges: the library holds codified federal law in depth, a selection of state high-court opinions, and notable federal dockets — but not state statutes, not local or municipal law, and not every document inside every docket (a filing only appears once someone has freed it from PACER). The law itself is public record; "public record" is not the same as "already digitized and free."

The sources, and the keys that open them

This explorer is independent and unaffiliated; it stands on open data and open APIs. Where a source asks for a key, the key is the operator's own, requested free from the provider:

  • Open Library / Internet Archive — books, covers, scans. No key.
  • Project Gutenberg & LibriVox — public-domain texts and audiobooks. No key.
  • OpenAlex — scholarly works. A contact email (OPENALEX_MAILTO) for the polite pool.
  • DOAB & OpenStax — open-access books and textbooks. No key.
  • CourtListener / RECAP (Free Law Project) — opinions and dockets. An API token (CL_TOKEN).
  • govinfo.gov (U.S. GPO) — U.S. Code, CFR, Public Laws. An api.data.gov key (GOVINFO_KEY).
  • arXiv — research papers, in full. No key.

Spines and covers

Nobody scans spines — only front covers — so the library binds its own. Each spine takes its color from its book's real cover, carries a vignette of the art at its head, and sets the title down the spine in type. Approach a shelf and the bindings resolve; open a book for the full jacket, the publication facts, and the description.

Reading

Millions of these books are scanned, in three tiers. Full text: public-domain works open complete, every page. Preview: in-copyright scans show a limited preview here; a free archive.org account lets you borrow the whole book — that gate is copyright law, not a missing key. Metadata only: no scan exists anywhere yet. Each book is labeled before you open it, and the English-language edition is preferred where one exists. Public-domain texts open right here as typeset, illustrated pages — and many also carry a free human-narrated audiobook from LibriVox: press Listen and the narration follows you through the stacks.

A living library

Unlike its impossible sibling, this library changes — but on its own terms. When the self-hosted index is connected, the shelves are pinned to a monthly snapshot of the catalog (named on the landing page), so every address holds until the next snapshot. The durable bookmark across snapshots is the book itself — its ISBN or Open Library ID.

Carry it with you

The library installs as an app — full screen, on your home screen or desktop, no browser chrome.

Lineage

Catalog data, covers, and reading are provided by the sources named above; this explorer is independent and unaffiliated with any of them. It shares its architecture with a companion piece: a true-scale Library of Babel containing every possible book. Between the two libraries lies the difference between everything that could be written and everything that has been — and, in the wings, everything that has been argued, enacted, and discovered.
